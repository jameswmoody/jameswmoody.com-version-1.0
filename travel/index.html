Travel

Where I've Been

A few years ago, I set a goal for myself. Up until then, I had barely visited a different timezone, let alone the rest of the world. I had recently turned 21 and decided now was the time to get out and see everything I still can. I decided I'd set the somewhat modest goal of visting all 50 states before I turn 30. As you can see below, I'm getting pretty close but I still have a few more to go.

I've also stared making a few internation trips as well. So far that includes Korea, Japan, Mexico and Canada, but I am hoping to add a lot more to the list soon.

United States

Detroit


Since I grew up in metro Detroit, it might not count as a travel destination. Technicalities aside, anyone who knows me knows I never shut up about this place. From coney dogs to Motown, to fully understand who I am you'd have to understand what Detroit is. Here's some pictures to help you get started.


Chicago


Nearly a decade ago, I moved to Chicago for college and have yet to leave. Who could blame me, there's tons of stuff to keep you interested for a while. It's like New York with less assholes, better pizza, and slightly more reasonable rent. The sports are OK here too sometimes.


New York City


My first visit to NYC was in 2003. Since then I’ve been there quite a few times and it’s interesting to see the changes over the nearly two decades since my first visit. It might not have the same edge it had in the 70’s, 80’s, and 90’s but as long as Sylvia’s in Harlem still exists, I’ll always have a reason to go back.


Las Vegas


Surprisingly, I’ve only been to Vegas once. I think a big part of this is my fear that no Vegas trip will ever come close to the first time. Between penthouse parties at Planet Hollywood to my friend dropping a stripper at Palomino’s, to the depressing state of society that is the Golden Nugget at 6am, I think I might have Vegas-peaked too early.


Washington D.C.


The District of Columbia has a somewhat special place in my heart. At 10 years old on a school trip, it was the first major city I had ever visited. Also, during high school I thought for sure I'd be going to Howard. I don't know if I could've tolerated all the weird slang the locals use so maybe I made the right choice. At the end of 2011 I spent a few days there partaking in pre-New Year activities. Since this was pretty early on in the travel endeavors, I didn't have the foresight to take more pictures. Until I make my way back, you'll have to settle for these.


San Francisco


If it wasn't for the insane cost of living, SF would probably be the next place I'd call home. For such a small area, there is so much you could do in one day. From checking out the ocean at Lands End, hiking through the redwoods in Muir Woods, or taking a boat to Alcatraz, there are a wide range of things to keep you busy while in San Fran.


Los Angeles


In my younger days, I thought I'd like to call LA home. I think now that there are too many people there trying to "make it" for me to be there longer than a week or so. Don't get me wrong, I love visiting the place, but I would not want to live there. That said, some of my favorite place to stop at include Santa Monica, Sunset Blvd, the Compton Swap Meet and Randy's Donuts. Seriously, they have some good ass donuts.


Memphis


Beale Street 2012....haven't been able to drink whiskey since. Found lots of soul and BBQ, but lost my liver somewhere.


Atlanta


ATL Shawty! Sorry….couldn’t resist. I flew down to the A in 2013 and ate double my weight in down-home southern cooking. Mary Mac’s, The Silver Skillet, Busy Bee. I’m pretty sure I exclusively drank sweet tea the entire time. Atlanta also had some of the nicest people I ever have come across while traveling. The Coca-Cola also taste fresher, FYI.


Gary


Gary sort of pissed me off. I'd found through a Google search that Gary, IN had a Coney Island (see the Detroit gallery above) and made an almost hour drive from Chicago to get my hands on a coney dog. I’d learn that not only was there no coney dog, there was nothing about the place that warranted painting Coney Island on the building. There was a consolation prize, however. I was able to stop by Michael Jackson’s childhood home.


St. Louis


St. Louis has a lot of weird people. I mean that in the most loving way possible. Seriously, I saw a lot of weird, random stuff in this city. But I'll say it has a unique character unlike any other place I've been so far. There also was a ton of public art here. You don't really see a ton of that in the Midwest. The highlight was the City Museum which is essentially a warehouse/playground/junkyard/giant art exibit. My pictures won't do it justice so do yourself a favor and Google it.


Indianapolis


In 2013, I stopped through Indy on the way to Louisville around Christmas time. There was more to look at downtown than I expected, but it was still Indianapolis after all. My expectations were already pretty low. Not a terrible place to stop for a bite to eat on the way to somewhere else.


Louisville


I think the best thing about my visit to Louisville was stopping by the Muhammad Ali Center in Downtown Louisville. Not only were there a number of Ali's artifacts and belongings (a highlight being the torch he carried for the 1996 Olympics), but there is also a message of tolorance and acceptance amongst all races and religions.


Milwaukee


I've been to Milwaukee way too many times (6). Seriously, I will be just fine if I never end up in Milwaukee again. The last 3 times have been against my will and I always swear I'll never go back. Bad beer, bad basketball, bad everything. I'm sure there are a lot of nice people there, they just happen to live in a boring city.


San Diego


The fish tacos are everything. The zoo is overpriced. It's like L.A. without the unjustied egos and wannabes.


Phoenix


To be fair, most of my frustrations with Phoenix stem from Phoenix Sky Harbor Airport. Without fail, every experience with this airport has been a nightmare for me. From 6 hour delays to a major malfunction shortly after takeoff, I'm convinced this airport is cursed which is why I vowed to never fly through it again. Phoenix itself isn't too bad (mostly baseball and old people) but I likely won't be going back any time soon since there's no chance of me flying there.


Minneapolis


I've heard people say that visiting Minneapolis was a huge mistake for them. I think the key is to visit in the summer when it's not an ungodly temperature outside. I had a pretty good time during my visit in July 2014. Prince and Mall of America? Who could regret that?


Philadelphia


When I visited Philly in 2014, I was a bit worried about boredom since it was the first time I went on a trip solo. After randomly meeting a few cool people while bar hopping though, my concerns didn’t last long. I also stayed at a hostel for the first time which was an experience in it’s own right. Besides drunken randomness with strangers, it was also cool to see many historical sights and locations. The 'Rocky Steps' were pretty cool too, I guess.


Dallas


I didn’t know what to expect out of my first trip to Texas. Cowboy’s and 'Texas Chainsaw Massacre' doesn’t exactly appeal to me. It didn’t help that everyone I talked to before leaving seemed to hate Dallas specifically. None of them know what they are talking about though, Dallas is awesome. Besides some of the weirdest/coolest bars I’ve ever experienced in Deep Ellum, the Bishop Arts District had really interesting hole in the wall restaurants and mom-and-pop stores.


Fort Worth


While in Dallas, we decided to make the somewhat short drive over to Fort Worth to see what it had to offer. I didn’t know much about FW before we got there besides every episode of 'Cops' seems to have been filmed there. The Stockyards were OK and made for some great pictures but the real attraction was Babe’s Chicken on the way back.


Austin


We got to ATX about 2 weeks after SXSW had ended so I’m not sure if 6th St is always as live as it was or if it was just the right time of year. Either way, out of all of the 'party streets' in the country that I’ve been to (such as Beale or Division streets), 6th street was the best. The were people of all ages, races, and backgrounds there for one reason: to have a good time. No fights or drama, just good times. Later on, we stopped at Torchy’s Tacos where I had a ‘White Trash’ taco that featured potato salad on top of a strip of fried chicken as well as 'The Republican' which had jalapeño sausage, cheese and pico. The menu might sound a bit gimmicky at first but believe me, the food is amazing.


San Antonio


I knew San Antonio had a river walk before we got there but I had no idea just how much my mind would be blown by it. Seriously, no pictures will do it justice, you just have to be there to understand. I also did’t expect the Alamo to look so small. I’ve heard people say it before but it really does look a lot smaller in person. Also noteworthy: The Saltlick. This place was in the middle of nowhere Texas (yeah, THAT kind of Texas) but was an oasis of smoked meats in a desert of ranches and 'Merica-ness. They saw me struggling to get a good picture of the smoke pit so they let me come around the counter to get a good shot from the kitchen. You can find it in this album so take a look if you want to see what meat heaven looks like.


Houston


Hmm, what nice things can I say about Houston?…Well, my favorite rapper from the south, Scarface, is from there so that’s pretty cool. I’d be hard pressed to find anything else interesting about this place. To Houston’s credit, I feel like I may have liked it a bit better if I had family or friends there. To a complete outsider though, it’s a boring, hot, moist place with mosquitos the size of humming birds.


Silicon Valley


During my last week working at Apple in 2015, I decided to book a flight out to Cupertino to check out HQ as an employee while I still could. They gave me a badge and I had free range to all of the internal public areas of the campus. After leaving, I decided to also walk through Google’s campus which is much more open and accessible than Apple’s (go figure). I ended the trip by stopping by other cities around Silicon Valley such as Sunnyvale, Palo Alto, and Redwood City. I ended the trip by hanging around downtown San Jose, drinking at a few bars and eating a burger and shake at Johnny Rockets.


Kansas City


If you can’t already tell from some of places I’ve written about, I love BBQ. In addition to my 50 states goal, I also have a sub-mission of traveling to all of the famous BBQ cities and seeing which is the best. As you can see, I’ve been to Memphis, Austin, Nashville, and St. Louis so far. As of yet, Kansas City has the best BBQ in my opinion of taste. There is a heavy focus on sauce here, which is my favorite part about BBQ so it was only natural that KC would win with me. I was there during Memorial Day Weekend which also happens to be one of the best BBQ holidays.


Des Moines


We didn’t spend much time in Des Moines, we were just passing through on the way back to Chicago from Kansas City. I had never been to Iowa before though, so I had to make a stop. There was as street festival going on in West Des Moines, possibly the most Main Street America-iest place that exists. After checking that out, we stopped at a barcade downtown that was surprisingly packed for 2pm on a Sunday. That’s just Iowa I guess.


Cleveland


As a Detroiter I have a natural-born hatred for anything Ohio, but even more-so for anything Cleveland. Still, it was a place I hadn’t been before so we made the drive there and stayed for a weekend. Great Lakes Brewery, East 99th/St. Clair, and an Indians game were all points of interest, but the biggest highlight of the trip was visiting the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame. The name is a bit deceiving since this museum covers nearly every genre of American music. I saw artifacts from all types of artists such as Jimi Hendrix, Biggie Smalls, Funkadelic, The Beatles, Michael Jackson, and Kurt Cobain to name a few. The only thing that could have made this place better was if it were in a different state. Did I mention I hate Ohio?


Mounument Valley


I’ve always had a weird fascination with being in the desert. Maybe it’s because it’s such a big contrast from the Midwest where I have spent my entire life. Visiting Monument Valley had been on my bucket list for a few years when I decided to finally make it happen. Two flights and a three hour car ride later, I was finally there. I spent the next four days drifting through the desert in a Mustang exploring everything between Flagstaff, AZ and Moab, UT. I also had to stop at the Four Corners Monument where AZ, UT, NM, and CO all meet.


Nashville


My girlfriend and I decided to take a road trip down to Nashville before it would get too cold to travel anywhere. It was here that I was able to experience hot chicken for the first time (look it up) as well as the country bar Honky Tonk. I’m not too big on country music but I wasn’t about to let that stop me from an authentic Nashville trip. The Grand Ole Opry hotel and mall were also more interesting than I expected.


Oakland


Although I had been to SF a few times, I hadn’t made my way across the Bay to Oakland. This time, I made sure to at least spend a few hours there. Granted, most of this time was spent at Lake Merritt but we also spent some time downtown exploring different shops and a bar or two. I also really liked all the graffiti (not sarcasm).


Portland


I’d always heard good things about Portland and finally got to check things out for myself. Sure there are a lot of hipsters, but they seemed a lot less pretentious than the ones you find in Brooklyn or East Austin. I was having a great time checking out dusty record stores, eating Voodoo Donuts, and walking through rose gardens when I seriously sprained my ankle while hiking on a hill. It was a downer on the rest the trip but I tried my best to keep it from holding me back.


Seattle


We stopped through Seattle on the way back from Vancouver. It was interesting seeing the birthplace of the grunge and Starbucks movements. Seattle had a certain feeling about the place. It was a gloomy overcast with off and on rain the entire time we were there but there was a strong since of embracement of the gloominess and seeing the beauty in it. It was also the Forth of July so we were able to watch the fireworks from the Space Needle which gave us a 360 degree view of all the firework displays across the area.


Omaha


Before I left for a weekend visit to Omaha, everyone I told had the same question for me — WHY? I’ve never needed much of an excuse to visit a place I’ve never been so for me the relatively short drive to Omaha was a no-brainer. I want to see everything I possibly can, even the things that might not be considered typical or interesting. That said, I had a good time in Omaha. I also discovered for myself one of the best IPA’s I’ve ever tasted from Zipline Brewery.


International

Korea


Before visiting Korea in early 2015, I had never really been outside of the country. Sure I had visited a few close places in Canada, but growing up 5 minutes away from the border makes it even less impressive than it already is. My first stop was in Seoul but I also took a train to other major cities like Busan and Daegu. It took some getting used to the food here but eventually I found myself craving kimchi with every meal. We also stopped by the DMZ which is the border between North and South Korea. It was weird how Disneyland-like everything felt there, especially considering the two countries are still technically at war. I snuck a few photos of North Korea while I was there.


Japan


On the way back to the US from Korea, I had a short layover in Japan. However, the plane that was supposed to pick me up from Tokyo had been delayed due to a snowstorm in NYC. Instead of being stuck at the airport for an extra four hours, I asked the airline if they could put me on the flight for the next day instead. They had no problem with this so I had a full 24 hours to spend exploring Tokyo.


Mexico


Rather than spending time at a resort in Cabo or Guadalajara, I wanted my first trip to Mexico to be at least a bit more authentic of an experience. Luckily I had a friend who was a bit more familiar with Mexico City and spoke Spanish. He ended up acting as our sherpa as we walked the streets of Distrito Federal. We also were able to visit the pyramids and a gift shop disguised as a tequila farm. We also club hopped through the nightlight section of town the last few nights.
